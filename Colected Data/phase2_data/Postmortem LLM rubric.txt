Postmortem Accountability-Grading Principles

You are grading a public technical postmortem using a theory-driven accountability rubric.

The goal is not to judge whether the company is morally good or whether the postmortem is objectively truthful. The goal is to score how strongly the postmortem text publicly represents accountability.

Accountability means: the organization makes the failure legible, attributable, and repairable to affected users or readers.

For every rubric item:

Read the postmortem carefully.

Assign only one score.

Use only evidence from the postmortem text.

Include one short evidence quote for each score.

Do not compare the postmortem to another company in the rationale.

Do not reward information that is implied but not stated.

Score the public text, not what the company may have done privately.

Core Principles

Principle 1: Disclosure of Harm

Accountability requires disclosing the scale of harm.

A postmortem is more accountable when it tells readers who or what was affected, how many users/customers/requests/systems were affected, how long the impact lasted, and how severe the degradation was.

This principle maps to:

Item 1 — Quantification of User Impact

Score based on whether user-facing impact is quantified.

0 = Impact is described only qualitatively.

Examples: “some users,” “degraded experience,” “quality issues,” “felt slower.”

1 = Some numbers are provided, but impact remains only partially quantified.

Examples: duration only, or one isolated count, without scope/magnitude.

2 = Impact is quantified across multiple dimensions.

Examples: affected users/customers, traffic share, request failure rate, latency, duration, geographic scope, service scope, data loss count.

Evidence to look for:

outage duration

affected-user count

customer/site count

traffic percentage

error rate

latency

number of failed requests

amount of lost data

number of affected services or regions

Principle 2: Temporal Answerability

Accountability requires exposing what the organization knew, when it knew it, and how quickly it responded.

A postmortem is more accountable when readers can reconstruct detection, escalation, mitigation, and recovery timing.

This principle maps to:

Item 2 — Internal-Awareness Timeline

Score based on whether the document gives a reconstructable timeline.

0 = Minimal timeline.

Only broad dates or vague ordering are given.

Reader cannot compute time-to-detect or time-to-mitigate.

1 = Partial timeline.

Deploy/revert dates or broad windows are given.

Uses vague timing such as “soon after,” “later that day,” “over a week.”

Exact detection or mitigation timing is missing.

2 = Explicit timeline.

Gives timestamps or clearly bounded times for detection, escalation, mitigation, and/or recovery.

Reader can compute response gaps.

Evidence to look for:

incident start time

detection time

first alert/page time

escalation time

mitigation time

recovery/restoration time

deployment/revert windows

Principle 3: Inspectable Causality

Accountability requires making the causal pathway inspectable.

A postmortem is more accountable when it identifies the actor, team, artifact, configuration, code path, dependency, prompt, rule, deployment, or mechanism that caused the incident. Individual blame is not required.

This principle maps to:

Item 3 — Causal Specificity / Agency

Score based on whether the cause is concrete and inspectable.

0 = Abstract or passive causality.

Examples: “a bug caused the issue,” “an error was introduced,” “the system entered an inconsistent state.”

1 = Mixed specificity.

Some concrete artifacts or mechanisms are named, but many causal claims remain abstract.

2 = High specificity.

The document names the relevant artifact, mechanism, system, actor/team, config, code path, prompt, rule, or deployment.

The causal pathway is inspectable.

Evidence to look for:

specific code/config/prompt/rule

named service/component

deployment/change description

protocol/tool behavior

command/script

system interaction

named team/process path

Do not require a named individual. System-level specificity can score 2.

Principle 4: Systemic Responsibility

Accountability requires acknowledging the broader failure stack, not reducing the incident to one convenient root cause.

A postmortem is more accountable when it names interacting technical, procedural, organizational, testing, monitoring, training, rollout, communication, or support failures.

This principle maps to:

Item 4 — Failure Enumeration

Score based on whether the document enumerates contributing factors.

0 = No contributing-factor enumeration.

The document presents one or more incident causes but does not enumerate a broader failure stack.

1 = Limited enumeration.

Names 2–4 contributing factors for the same failure/degradation.

Mostly technical.

2 = Broad enumeration.

Names 5+ contributing factors.

Includes at least one procedural, organizational, monitoring, rollout, testing, training, support, or communication failure.

Evidence to look for:

“what went wrong” lists

contributing factors

5 Whys

root cause plus contributing causes

testing gaps

monitoring/alerting gaps

process/runbook gaps

communication/support gaps

rollback/recovery gaps

organizational ownership gaps

Do not give 2 for merely listing multiple symptoms. The factors should explain why the failure occurred, spread, persisted, or was hard to recover from.

Principle 5: Verifiable Repair

Accountability requires repair commitments that are concrete enough to be audited.

A postmortem is more accountable when its action items are specific, falsifiable, scoped, dated, assigned, measured, completed, or tied to named deliverables.

This principle maps to:

Item 5 — Action Item Concreteness

Score based on whether remediation is concrete and verifiable.

0 = Mostly vague action items.

Examples: “improve processes,” “strengthen controls,” “review systems,” “communicate better.”

1 = Concrete but weakly verifiable.

Names concrete technical or process changes.

But most lack dates, owners, metrics, completion status, acceptance criteria, or specific scope.

2 = Concrete and falsifiable.

Most action items name concrete changes and include at least two of the following:

deadline/ETA

completion status

owner/team

metric

count/scope

acceptance criterion

named deliverable

Evidence to look for:

“Done”

ETA/deadline

owner/team

exact system to be changed

migration target

rollout plan

monitoring/alert to be added

number of objects reviewed

named tool/process/runbook

measurable acceptance condition

Principle 6: Recognition of Affected Parties

Accountability requires recognizing affected users/customers as harmed parties, not just as sources of reports or feedback.

A postmortem is more accountable when it explicitly apologizes, accepts responsibility, expresses remorse, or offers restitution.

This principle maps to:

Item 6 — Stance Toward Harm Caused

Score based on how directly the document acknowledges harm and responsibility.

0 = No harm acknowledgment.

The document describes the technical failure but does not clearly acknowledge harm to users/customers.

Or it frames the response mainly as information-sharing, gratitude, or appreciation.

1 = Mild or implicit acknowledgment.

Acknowledges poor experience or disruption, but mildly.

Examples: “this is not the experience users should expect,” “we appreciate your patience,” “users were disrupted.”

2 = Explicit remorse or responsibility.

Expresses apology, remorse, shame, responsibility, or restitution.

Examples: “we are sorry,” “we apologize,” “we are ashamed,” “we take full responsibility,” “we know this hurt customers,” “we are issuing credits/refunds/resets.”

Evidence to look for:

apology

remorse/shame

explicit responsibility

“impact on customers”

restitution/credits/refunds/resets

recognition of disruption or lost work

Principle 7: Failure-Centered Framing

Accountability requires centering the failure itself, rather than centering discourse, rumors, complaints, or reports about the failure.

A postmortem is more accountable when the title and opening make the outage/degradation/incident the subject.

This principle maps to:

Item 7 — Framing Subject

Score based on the title and opening framing.

0 = Discourse-centered framing.

Centers reports, concerns, rumors, feedback, customer questions, or public conversation.

Examples: “An update on recent quality reports,” “Responding to customer concerns.”

1 = Failure-centered framing.

Centers the outage, degradation, incident, failure, postmortem, or return-to-service process.

Examples: “Details of the outage,” “Incident postmortem,” “Return to service,” “DDoS incident report.”

Evidence to look for:

title

subtitle

first paragraph

framing sentence

Optional Narrative Flags

These are not included in the numeric score. Mark them only if clearly present.

Flag A: Defensive Denial

Use when the document responds to an external accusation, suspected motive, or reputational claim rather than only explaining the engineering record.

Examples:

“We never intentionally degraded the model.”

“This was not caused by negligence.”

“Contrary to speculation…”

Output:

flag_type = Defensive denial

evidence_quote

rationale

Flag B: Embedded Marketing

Use when the document includes product promotion, model promotion, brand reinforcement, competitive positioning, or reliability advertising inside the postmortem.

Examples:

“Model X found the bug while Model Y did not.”

“Our world-class platform…”

“Our industry-leading reliability…”

Output:

flag_type = Embedded marketing

evidence_quote

rationale

Flag C: Responsibility Diffusion

Use when the document disperses responsibility into vague systems, users, conditions, or reports while avoiding clear internal causes.

Examples:

“Unexpected usage patterns revealed an issue.”

“Some users reported concerns.”

“A rare combination of factors occurred.”

Do not mark this flag if the document later gives a concrete internal cause.

Output:

flag_type = Responsibility diffusion

evidence_quote

rationale

If no flags apply, output:

flag_type = None

evidence_quote = empty

rationale = “No clear defensive denial, embedded marketing, or responsibility diffusion flag.”

Output Format

For each postmortem, output:

The link.

A CSV block with this exact header:

document_id,item_number,item_name,principle,score,evidence_quote,rationale

One row for each of the seven items.

One total row.

The total row should use:

item_number = total

item_name = Total score

principle = empty

score = sum of all item scores out of 13

evidence_quote = empty

rationale = concise rubric-internal explanation

Do not compare the document to another company in the total rationale.

Good total rationale examples:

“High score driven by quantified impact, explicit timeline, causal specificity, broad failure enumeration, concrete remediation, harm acknowledgment, and failure-centered framing.”

“Mixed score driven by strong causal specificity and failure-centered framing, with weaker quantified impact, failure enumeration, action-item verifiability, and harm acknowledgment.”

“Low score driven by qualitative impact description, partial timeline, limited failure enumeration, and discourse-centered framing.”

Bad total rationale examples:

“More political than Cloudflare.”

“Less accountable than Roblox.”

“Better than Anthropic.”

Then output optional flags in a second CSV block with this exact header:

document_id,flag_type,evidence_quote,rationale

Scoring Reminder

Total possible score: 13.

Items 1–6 are scored 0, 1, or 2.Item 7 is scored 0 or 1.

Interpretation:

9–13 = high accountability-framing score

7–8 = mixed accountability-framing score

0–6 = low accountability-framing score

This score measures accountability framing in the public text, not the company’s actual internal accountability or moral responsibility.
